Glossary

Terms Definition
Pulmonary Regulation The control and maintenance of levels of CO2 and O2 in the blood through the respiratory system
Renal Regulation Reabsorption of bicarbonate and other chemicals in the kidneys, which filters the blood stream and removes waste/excess chemicals
pH A measure of acidity or alkalinity of a solution, expressed on a scale from 0 to 14. Values above 7 are basic, values below 7 are acidic and values at or close to 7 are neutral
Blood buffer A biological system within our bodies with the purpose maintaining a stable pH level for our blood (ph of 7.35-7.45)
Hyperventilation Abnormally rapid and deep breathing, leading to a decrease in carbon dioxide levels in the blood, and potentially causing respiratory alkalosis
Hypoventilation Abnormally slow and shallow breathing, resulting in an accumulation of carbon dioxide in the blood, potentially causing respiratory acidosis
Metabolic Acidosis A condition characterized by an increased acidity in the blood, often due to the accumulation of acids or the loss of bicarbonate.
Metabolic Alkalosis A condition characterized by an increased alkalinity in the blood, often due to the loss of acids or an excessive intake of alkaline substances.
Hypovolemic shock A severe condition where there is a large decrease in blood volume, leading to inadequate perfusion of tissues and organs
Lungs The primary respiratory organs responsible for gas exchange, where oxygen is taken up and carbon dioxide is eliminated from the body.
Kidneys Vital organs responsible for the filtration of blood, regulation of electrolytes, maintenance of fluid balance, and excretion of waste products through urine.
Red Blood Cells Responsible for delivering oxygen to the tissues in the body. As well as transporting carbon dioxide by binding to bicarbonate in the blood cell
Equilibrium A state where the rate of the forward reaction is equal to the rate of the backward reaction. When the reaction has reached a state of balance and stability
Acid-Base Balance Balance between input/production and output/elimination of hydrogen ions. Your blood needs this balance to function properly.
Weak Acid Acids that don’t completely dissociate in solution.
Buffering Capacity A solution’s ability to resist changes in PH through absorption/desorbing H+ and OH- ions.
Electrolytes Substances that conduct electricity as a result of dissociating into positive and negative electrical charge when dissolved in water.
Hemoglobin A protein molecule found in red blood cells that helps transport oxygen and carbon dioxide.
